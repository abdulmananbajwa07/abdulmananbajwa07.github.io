## VERSION 1 — PERSONAL WEBSITE

When I first sat down to prepare for the Azure AI Engineer (AI-102) exam, I'll be honest—I was nervous. It's one of Microsoft's most comprehensive certifications, covering everything from Azure Cognitive Services to large language models. But six weeks later, after structured study and hands-on labs, I walked out of the testing centre with a passing score. Today, I want to share what actually worked for me and help you avoid the pitfalls I hit along the way.

### Why Azure AI Engineer Matters Now

The AI-102 certification validates your ability to design and implement end-to-end AI solutions on Azure. Unlike simpler certifications, this one demands *real* technical depth—you're not just learning APIs, you're understanding when to use Azure OpenAI vs. Azure AI Search, how to build RAG pipelines, and how to implement responsible AI practices. If you work with AI, this credential opens doors to senior roles and projects that matter.

That said, it's challenging. The exam covers 11 major domains, from foundational ML concepts to production deployments. But with the right strategy, it's absolutely achievable.

### The 11 Domains You Need to Know

The exam tests knowledge across these areas:

1. **Plan and manage an Azure AI solution** — Governance, compliance, resource planning
2. **Implement decision-making solutions** — Anomaly detection, time series forecasting
3. **Implement content moderation solutions** — Content safety and filtering
4. **Implement computer vision solutions** — Image analysis, object detection, OCR
5. **Implement natural language processing (NLP) solutions** — Text analytics, entity recognition
6. **Implement knowledge mining solutions** — Building indexes with Azure AI Search
7. **Implement generative AI solutions** — Azure OpenAI, prompt engineering, RAG
8. **Implement responsible AI practices** — Fairness, transparency, accountability
9. **Monitor and optimize solutions** — Logging, alerting, cost optimization
10. **Build speech and translation solutions** — Speech-to-text, translation APIs
11. **Build document intelligence solutions** — Form Recognition, layout analysis

The exam is 60 questions in 120 minutes—roughly 2 minutes per question. Some are straightforward recall; others are scenario-based and require critical thinking.

### My Study Strategy (What Actually Worked)

**Weeks 1–2: Foundation Phase**
I started with the Microsoft Learn modules—all of them. Yes, it's time-consuming, but they're free and align directly with the exam objectives. I took notes in Obsidian, grouping by domain. The goal wasn't to memorize; it was to understand *concepts* and when they apply.

**Weeks 3–4: Hands-On Labs**
This is where most people slip up. Reading about Azure AI Search is not the same as building a RAG pipeline. I built six mini-projects:
- A document intelligence solution to extract structured data
- A Q&A chatbot using Azure OpenAI + AI Search
- A computer vision app for image tagging
- A sentiment analysis pipeline
- An anomaly detector for time-series data
- A speech translation microservice

Each took 2–3 hours, but they cemented my understanding. When the exam asks "Which service would you use for X?", you've *done* X, so the answer is obvious.

**Weeks 5: Practice Exams**
I used two sources:
- **Udemy Practice Exams** by John Savill (~200 questions, realistic difficulty)
- **Microsoft Learn Practice Tests** (free, slightly easier but good for gaps)

I scored 72% on my first attempt, 81% on my second. Each wrong answer became a learning point. I tracked weak areas (mine was speech services—I hadn't used them much) and drilled those topics.

**Week 6: Final Review**
The week before the exam, I didn't cram. Instead, I reviewed my weak domains, redid two practice exams, and made sure I understood the *why*, not just the *what*. I also tested some hands-on scenarios in the Azure portal to stay sharp.

### What the Exam Actually Feels Like

Here's the real talk: the questions are harder than they look. Many are worded to test nuance. For example:

> "You're building a chatbot. The customer asks a question that your knowledge base doesn't cover. What should you happen?"

The obvious answer might be "return a default response," but the exam wants you to think about fallback strategies, confidence scores, and routing to human agents. Read *carefully*.

About 40% of my exam was scenario-based questions where you're given a real-world situation and need to choose the best architecture or solution. They reward practical thinking over memorization.

There are also drag-and-drop questions where you order steps in a workflow. Practice these—they can trip you up if you haven't seen them before.

### Key Takeaways

- **Learn the why, not just the what.** Understand *when* to use each service, not just that it exists.
- **Build mini-projects in weeks 3–4.** Hands-on experience is non-negotiable for this exam.
- **Practice exams are your best friend.** Aim for 75–80% on practice tests before the real thing.
- **Manage test anxiety.** I flagged 15 questions and returned to them at the end with fresh eyes. This saved at least 2 points.
- **Focus on weak domains.** Don't spend time on topics you already know well. Drill your gaps ruthlessly.

### Wrapping Up

If you're considering AI-102, go for it. It's challenging, but it's also the most rewarding certification I've pursued. You'll finish with real, deployable skills—not just badge karma. The investment of 6 weeks pays dividends in your career.
